Improve your school’s cyber security resilience and protect data using the 3-2-1 backup strategy.

What is the 3-2-1 backup strategy

3-2-1 backup is a data protection strategy that recommends having:

  • 3 copies of your data (one primary and two backups)
  • 2 different forms of storage (such as on-premise and cloud)
  • 1 off-site backup (such as a remote data centre or cloud)

Why it is important

The 3-2-1 backups are essential for:

  • safeguarding student data 
  • operational continuity  
  • compliance  

Following the strategy can mitigate risks such as cyber attacks, accidental deletions, natural disasters and IT failures. In addition, they can help with the continuity of education when needed.

These risks include:

Cyber attacks

Permanent off-site backups ensure data recovery without having to pay ransom.

Accidental deletion 

Multiple copies allow fast recovery of files that have been lost due to human error.   

Server crashes and hardware failure

Redundant storage options ensure minimal disruption. 

Cloud service outages  

Local backups provide access even if cloud services fail. 

Natural disasters (such as fire and floods)  

Off-site backups ensure data is protected from local disasters.  

Choosing a platform for backing up  

Before selecting a platform for your backups, you should be aware that Microsoft and most cloud services operate on a shared responsibility model.  

This means they ensure their platform availability, but this does not fully protect you from data loss due to accidental deletion, cyber attacks, or compliance issues.     

Microsoft provides guidance on its backup functions.     

Unlike traditional on-premises infrastructure, cloud environments present unique challenges, such as:     

  • limited built-in retention, for example, deleted files are only retained for 93 days in SharePoint and OneDrive
  • ransomware attacks can encrypt cloud data, including OneDrive and SharePoint files
  • accidental deletions or insider threats leading to data loss that service retention policies cannot recover beyond a certain period
  • legal and compliance requirements such as UK GDPR and Ofsted data requirements which may require long-term retention of sensitive records

NCSC guidance on backup best practice 

National Cyber and Security Centre (NCSC) has developed a set of “rules” to follow when backing up your data.

The 3-2-1 Rule 

NCSC stresses the importance of the 3-2-1 backup strategy, stating it is vital to keep multiple backups and to logically separate them.

By maintaining resilient backups, if one backup is compromised at least one other remains.

The Offline Rule: Disconnect at least one backup

Keeping one backup offline will prevent ransomware encrypting all backups.

You should also:

  • digitally disconnect cloud backups not in use
  • use separate ID and password credentials for cloud backups
  • restrict access so that only devices specifically authorised by your IT security manager, such as admin workstations, can modify backups

The Recovery Rule: Ensure your backups can be restored

Schools must confirm that backups are recoverable in case of an attack. When choosing a cloud storage provider, you should ensure that:

  • the version history is available to restore previous versions of files
  • deleted files can be recovered for a set period after removal
  • regular tests of restoring backups are carried out to ensure data integrity

The Regular Rule: Frequent and tested backups

The final rule is the frequency of backing up your data. Schools should back up data on a regular and agreed upon schedule to minimise data loss. Backups should be:

  • tested frequently to confirm they work as intended
  • automated where possible to ensure consistency 

Steps for applying 3-2-1 in a cloud-only environment

If your school relies on a cloud-based system such as Office 365, the NCSC recommends you still follow the 3-2-1 strategy. This will ensure that even if one backup is compromised, others remain intact.  Follow the steps below to implement the strategy.

Step 1: Identifying and backing up critical data

Schools should ensure they identify and back up their most critical data which includes:

  • student & staff emails (Exchange Online)
  • files & collaboration documents (OneDrive & SharePoint)
  • Microsoft Teams data (chats, shared files, meeting recordings, Google meets)
  • school administrative data (MIS integration, financial documents)
  • safeguarding records (stored in secure SharePoint sites or OneDrive)

Step 2: Implement a third-party backup solution for Office 365

Since Microsoft does not provide native full backups, it is recommended that a dedicated Microsoft 365 backup solution is used to ensure recoverability.

Select a Software as a Service (SaaS) based backup provider that best meets your needs. You should ensure the solution supports:

  • automated backups (daily or more frequent)
  • granular recovery (individual emails, Teams messages, SharePoint files)
  • long-term retention (up to 7 years for compliance)
  • immutable storage (protection against ransomware encryption)

Storage options can be either:

  • a cloud-to-cloud backup such as backing up M365 data to a separate cloud provider
  • a hybrid approach by storing an encrypted backup locally in a secure Network Attached Storage (NAS) for redundancy

Step 3: Implement an off-site, air-gapped backup (third Copy - Disaster Recovery Protection)

For true 3-2-1 resilience, store one copy in air-gapped storage that is separate from daily operations. This should have limited accessibility but be reachable when needed.

Options for air-gapped backups include:

  • commercial cloud services with write-once-read-many (WORM) features to ensure ransomware protection
  • physical air-gapped storage (e.g., an offline encrypted external drive updated monthly)

Further guidance on data backup

The following NCSC guidance provides information on the different types of backups and the importance of:

  • offline backups in an online world
  • ransomware-resistant backups
  • using online services safely
