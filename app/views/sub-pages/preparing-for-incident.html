Planning for resilience with a Cyber Response Plan

Education settings should be prepared to respond swiftly and effectively to cyber security incidents. A critical document that can help build resilience is your Cyber Response Plan (CRP). A CRP is a tactical document that coordinates all response activities the process your organisation should follow in the event of a suspected incident. 

The DfE provides a CRP template alongside guidance on how to tailor the CRP to best meet your setting’s needs. If you already have a CRP in place, you can use the guidance to review the content in your existing plan.  

It’s also good practice to have a Cyber Security Incident Management Policy (CSIMP), a strategic document that covers the management of cyber security incidents and sets the high-level approach, including roles and responsibilities. You may already have a generic Incident Policy or Disaster Recovery Plan which includes cyber security incidents in place.  

Tailoring your response plan

 

It is essential that every organisation tailors its CRP to its own context, requirements, and capabilities. A plan that is too generic or vague risks missing critical actions, leaving responders uncertain about what to do if when an incident occurs. If a plan is over-engineered or has been duplicated from another organisation, it risks becoming complex or unrealistic. 

A successful CRP should be detailed enough to guide a confident and coordinated response, yet practical and aligned with the organisation’s structure, resources, and ways of working.  

Involve the right people

IT staff, senior leaders, HR and communications teams and key suppliers or IT support providers should be involved in the plan preparation. Senior leadership will support the plan and understand its implications, especially when serious disruption or reputational harm is possible. This support will be documented in the CSIMP.  

You can find further guidance in the cyber security standards for schools and colleges.

Be aware of other plans

To ensure responses are integrated and consistent, you should link your incident response documents to any existing plans, policies and procedures, such as your:

  • Disaster Recovery Plan for technical recovery
  • Business Continuity Plan for maintaining the delivery of education
  • Safeguarding policy for incidents involving learners or child protection
  • Data Protection Policy for incidents involving the potential loss of personal information
  • Backup Policy to outline your backup procedure and potential scenarios
  • Communication Strategy to know who should be contacted first and potentially how to handle communication with a hacker
  • Service Level Agreements that show agreements with IT or other key service providers
  • Acceptable Use Policy to provide guidance to users

The digital and technology standards provide further guidance on disaster recovery and business continuity plans.  

Know your legal duties

Your plan should include a brief guide to any legal or regulatory reporting requirements such as to the Information Commissioner’s Office or the DfE. This should be proportionate to the type and volume of personal or sensitive data held and the scale of the potential incident.     

Important foundations of incident response planning

Our cyber response guidance provides technical assistance in responding to incidents as they occur. The response process is underpinned by the foundational incident management activities listed below.

Define roles and responsibilities

Clear roles should be assigned to each person who will be involved in responding to the plan. This includes assigning an Incident Lead and decision-makers in relevant areas such as IT, facilities and safeguarding.  

The roles should be understood, documented, and supported with training where necessary. Deputies should be named in the event of absence, with all contact details kept up to date.   

You should begin by establishing a Cyber Recovery Team led by a SLT Digital Lead.

SLT Digital Lead

The SLT Digital Lead is the educational setting’s responsible stakeholder for overseeing the management of cyber security incidents and owning incident management.  

Depending on the context in each educational setting, the SLT Digital Lead may delegate responsibility for cyber security incident management. This role is concerned with leadership and management of incidents, not necessarily the technical responsibilities.  

Key responsibilities are to:

  • lead and coordinate the CRP ensuring timely delegation of tasks and appropriate escalation
  • act as the single point of contact for incident management decisions and includes reporting to senior management
  • liaise with IT support and others to ensure appropriate response tasks are initiated and monitored as directed by the CRP or playbook
  • activate and oversee the Cyber Recovery Team as required by the type and severity of the incident
  • lead Post-Incident Review activities to ensure any issues are identified and lessons learnt are integrated into cyber security policy or plan updates and other documents such as cyber risk assessments
  • maintain linkage between key stakeholders such as the Cyber Recovery Team, IT support and Designated Safeguarding Lead

Cyber Recovery Team

The Cyber Recovery Team is a multi-disciplinary group of key personnel, coordinated by the SLT Digital Lead. They are responsible for carrying out technical and non-technical response activities.  

Key roles within your organisation that should be aware and involved are:

  • SLT Digital Lead
  • Incident Lead as appropriate for your setting
  • Technical stakeholders such as your IT team and managed provider Stakeholders
  • IT team
  • ICT Managed Provider
  • Data Protection Officer (DPO)
  • Designated Safeguarding Lead
  • Senior Leadership Team (SLT)
  • Communications Lead
  • Legal/Governance Lead if applicable

In smaller settings it is likely that individuals will carry out multiple roles which should be assigned to fit your setting’s requirements. The NCSC advise that a setting should always consider the risk of individuals being unavailable and to include at least two contact methods for two or more individuals.  

Depending on the impact and resulting damages of an incident occurring, parties outside of your education setting may have to become involved, which may include:

  • your IT provider
  • your insurance provider 
  • Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO)
  • NCSC
  • legal guardians
  • local police

Communicate

Alongside helping you respond effectively, communicating with the CRT and relevant stakeholders will help protect your setting’s infrastructure, data and reputation. You can find guidance on communicating externally in [x]. 

The SLT Digital Lead will oversee communications but may delegate responsibilities to others depending on the size of setting.  

Preplanning internal communications in the event of an incident should include identifying what communications are likely to be necessary before, during and after a significant cyber security incident.   

Being prepared will help the education setting conduct effective and timely communications, providing reassurance both internally and externally. The NCSC has produced guidance to help education settings manage their communications strategy before, during and after a cyber security incident.    

Empower

Set escalation criteria

You should define what types of incidents must be escalated to senior leaders and how quickly, using the impact on learning, safeguarding, data security or operational continuity to determine severity. This also includes contacting the RPA if a setting is part of the scheme.    

Use playbooks and check authorisations 

As part of your CRP preparation, you should create and maintain playbooks for common incidents such as phishing, ransomware, and data loss.    

Relevant staff should know how to access the playbooks and have awareness of who can authorise key actions, such as disabling systems and notifying relevant third parties. Suppliers should also be made aware of these authorisations.    

Swift containment    

You should define where IT Support can be granted the permission to act without authorisation to reduce risk and likely impact, such as disconnecting systems to prevent spread.
