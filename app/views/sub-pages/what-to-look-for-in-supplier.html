Evaluating cyber security suppliers thoroughly will ensure they meet the required standards and reduce the likelihood of a supply chain attack.

Principles of supply chain security

Both the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) and the National Protective Security Authority (NPSA) provide substantial guidance about supplier security and protecting an organisation’s supply chain.

Principles 4-9 of the NCSC’s supply chain security guidance looks at the need to establish control:  


{% call blueContentBox() %}

Principles 4-9:

  1. Communicate your view of security needs to your suppliers
  2. Set and communicate minimum security requirements for your suppliers
  3. Build security considerations into your contracting processes and require that your suppliers do the same
  4. Meet your own security responsibilities as a supplier and consumer
  5. Raise awareness of security within your supply chain
  6. Provide support for security incidents
{% endcall %}

Approaches to setting and communicating minimum security requirements

The NCSC have case studies (NCSC) that illustrate the different approaches that can be taken when setting and communicating the minimum security requirements for suppliers (covered in Principle 5). They also cover how the measures used to address one use case can be re-used for others.

The NCSC has guidance that helps medium to large organisations build confidence in the security of their supply chain.

Challenges in granting IT suppliers access to your networks

In their guidance on IT supplier relationships, the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) explores some of the challenges to granting IT suppliers access to an organisation's networks and assets. They also cover key considerations for implementing processes for acquiring, using, managing and exiting cloud services.
