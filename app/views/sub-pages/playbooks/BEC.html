Overview

As defined by NCSC, a business email compromise (BEC) occurs “when a criminal accesses a work email account in order to trick someone into transferring money, or to steal valuable (or sensitive) data”.

This can involve any user accounts used to access and manage financial, personal or other sensitive information (including senior staff, human resources and regular staff with minimal privileges).  

Unlike ordinary phishing emails which typically target a large group of users, BEC emails are tailored and targeted to individuals within a setting. As users with access to sensitive materials are typically part of the Senior Leadership Team, this targeted approach can also be known as ‘whaling’ or ‘spear phishing’. 

The aim of this is to either impersonate a legitimate member of a setting to take advantage of their financial processes, allowing finances to be indirectly sent to the attacker or to gain access to the account to carry out further attacks.

Purpose

The purpose of this playbook is to provide technical insight and guidance into the actions to be considered when investigating and responding to a BEC incident.  

This document is designed for educational settings with some existing cyber security controls. It is not a standalone document and must be used in conjunction with your settings Incident Management Plan (Cyber Response Plan) and associated safeguarding procedures. 

Whilst this document will contain technical actions that can be taken during an incident, it should ideally be reviewed by your technical support team to tailor a response process specifically to your setting. This should be done in advance and kept up to date so that your tailored playbook is ready to be used when an incident occurs.

This document covers a BEC incident affecting a settings IT infrastructure, digital assets and data held within. It is designed to be used by your settings technical IT teams or ICT Managed service provider, alongside existing incident management (cyber response) and safeguarding procedures.  

This document will explain the issue at its highest level, meaning that every aspect may not be relevant to your setting. If you have any system specific issues or queries, these should be addressed to your technical, ICT Managed service provider and/or senior leadership team for additional guidance.

Immediate actions

If you have identified an ongoing attack, first consult your Cyber Response Plan (CRP). This will inform you on your setting’s standard cyber incident response process which this playbook will enhance. This includes the following initial response: 

  • Contact your service provider immediately as they will help with remediation efforts.
  • If you’ve identified a fraudulent payment, contact your bank directly using their official website or phone number.
  • This should also be reported as a crime to Action Fraud by calling 0300 123 2040.
  • If you are a member of the Risk Protection Agreement (RPA) please inform them of the incident.
  • If you have an insurance policy, your insurance company should be contacted. Depending on your insurance agreement, they may assist in remediation plans.

 In order to contain the infection and minimise impact, it is important your technical support or service provider carry out the following immediate actions:

  1. Contact your Digital and Incident SLT to enact your setting’s Cyber Response Plan. If you do not have a BEC specific plan, continue following this process.
    1. If you have been financially impacted, inform your settings bank to prevent further financial damage.
  2. Isolate the affected accounts and/or devices.
    1. If you have identified which accounts have been compromised, isolate these accounts by (Please follow Microsoft guidance)
      1. Restricting the ability to send emails (Outlook) to prevent further spread and ensure email forwarding has not be set up
      2. Restrict any additional privileges
      3. Forcefully disabling the user account
        1. This can be done by changing password and login authorisation 
      4. If possible, forcefully sign-out of all active sessions
      5. If your setting has multi-factor authentication (MFA) in place, ensure there are no unauthorised methods added to prevent reinfection 
    2. If a specific device has been impacted (for example, a keylogger virus was installed), then disconnect the device from your settings network as soon as possible. 
      1. This can be by disabling the internet connection of the device or if not possible, disabling the wi-fi to completely isolate the device
      2. Scan the network using an anti-virus to ensure there are no persistent malware on your system
  3. Ensure that the integrity of your settings financial systems (e.g. payroll, HR) have not been severely compromised. This includes:
    1. Administrative accounts
    2. User payroll information
    3. Expected payment amounts

Reporting

All cyber incidents should be reported immediately to the relevant authority. As a ransomware incident has likely resulted in some form of data breach, the following external supervisory authorities should be informed: 

Information Commissioners Office

This must be done within 72 hours of becoming aware of your incident/data breach. 

This includes the nature of the breach, categories and number of individuals data that has been affected.

National cyber security centre (NCSC)

If there is a clear danger to safeguarding for either staff members or leaners, please contact NCSC and fill out the form.

Local Police Authorities

Please reach out to your local police authority if there is suspected clear financial or safeguarding impact.

Action Fraud

You can visit their website or call 0300 123 2040

DfE RPA Team

If you are a member of the RPA membership scheme, please contact the RPA Emergency Assistance Helpline by emailing RPAresponse@CyberClan.com or call 0800 368 6378.

Service Providers

If the breach has affected any third-party service/system, the service providers should be informed of the attack and involved in a coordinated response to the incident. 

Insurance Providers 

If you are a member of a private insurance and cyber incidents are part of your coverage, please contact them immediately. Dependant on your coverage, they may provide direct assistance in recovery. 

In the event your incident has been discovered and reported by a news media outlet, it is important that you have assigned a staff member to co-ordinate communications in line with an approved post-disaster communication plan. The staff media contact should only provide verifiable information, with no room for speculation.  

For information that is still not known, it is justified to simply state “That is not known at this stage”. Only the appointed staff media contact should communicate with the media, with all other staff refusing to comment.

Indicators of Compromise

The following are examples of an indicator of compromise (IOC) for a business email compromise: 

Unusual emails/messages from Senior Leadership and financial teams around financial requests.

You should pay attention to:

  • changes in language, especially in tone and/or urgency
  • any requests to bypass normal procedures to access the finances in an insecure fashion.

You should always follow your settings financial processes and verify directly with a member of your financial team

Unusual payments made to unknown recipients.

These may be: 

  • outside of your setting’s regular payment schedule or window
  • from trusted suppliers that have been compromised

You should always ensure all payments are verified with your SLT, the user requesting payment and financial teams

Unauthorised changes to payroll information.

If a user has recently changed their payment information, verify these changes with the user prior to sending any payments

Login attempts to accounts from outside of working hours and expected geolocations.  You should pay attention to:

  • log in attempts from countries that have no relation to your setting
  • users attempting to access their accounts whilst away, ensuring that any activity is verified directly with the user
  • accounts being accessed outside of regular working hours, which may indicate malicious activity

Multiple failed login attempts

Users may sometime forget their logon information, so having a clear recovery process is important. 

If there are multiple consistent failed attempts, this may be an indication of a ‘brute force attack’, and that account should be disabled/isolated ASAP

Containment

Containment Procedures

In the event a Business Email Compromise incident takes place, it is key to prioritise accounts and system that may hold financial and/or personal information about people within your setting.

Verify that any accounts with key privileges are locked down and have not been infected. This can be done by auditing account access, verifying that only the legitimate user has access to their account. 

If your setting uses Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, Microsoft Defender XDR or Microsoft Defender Antivirus, it is possible that the account has been contained automatically. This may have occurred due to existing attack surface reduction rules or behavioural blocking, as the account was displaying suspicious behaviour. 

This can be manually done by further restricting the permissions of individual user accounts by doing the following: 

  • Disable the affected user account
  • If you are unable to disable the account, the next best option is to reset the password.
  • Revoke user access
  • This can be done using Microsoft Entra ID.
  • Review multi-factor authentication (MFA) registered devices
  • Review administrative roles assigned to the user
  • Review mail forwarders

Recovery

Recovery Actions

Depending on what types of accounts your setting uses, there are multiple ways to recover a potentially compromised account. 

Be sure to contact your service provider to see if they can assist in recovery, otherwise the account provider should be contacted directly. 

Depending on your email account provider there will be specific actions to take. For example:

  • Microsoft may have already locked the account for suspicious behaviour. If this has happened please reach out to your service provider to unlock and secure the account, or reach out directly to Microsoft.
  • If a user’s Google account has been locked, either through suspicious behaviour or the user has locked it themselves, please contact your service provider or Google.

Post-Incident

Debrief

It is important that all members of staff involved in incident investigation, triaging, mitigation and recovery undergo some form of interview and action review to ensure all the appropriate steps were taken to mitigate the incident.

This includes communicating to all stakeholders internal to your setting (e.g. Digital Leader, Senior Leadership, safeguarding officer), and external to your investigation if they have been impacted (e.g. parent/learner data will require legal guardians to be informed “without undue delay” according to GDPR).

Ensure your incident review report is readable and concise to help present to senior leadership. It is key to have full support in the actions taken during and post-incident as to prevent a ‘blame culture’ and ensure that the right steps were taken.  

It is important to remember that whilst the initial incident has been mitigated and system recovered, all users and incident leader should remain vigilant and aware of possible future compromise. This should be involved in any debriefs, training activities and lead to a review/update of your setting cyber response plan and cyber risk assessment.
