Overview

As defined by NCSC, a business email compromise (BEC) occurs “when a criminal accesses a work email account in order to trick someone into transferring money, or to steal valuable (or sensitive) data”.

This can involve any user accounts used to access and manage financial, personal or other sensitive information (including senior staff, human resources and regular staff with minimal privileges).  

Unlike ordinary phishing emails which typically target a large group of users, BEC emails are tailored and targeted to individuals within a setting. As users with access to sensitive materials are typically part of the Senior Leadership Team, this targeted approach can also be known as ‘whaling’ or ‘spear phishing’. 

The aim of this is to either impersonate a legitimate member of a setting to take advantage of their financial processes, allowing finances to be indirectly sent to the attacker or to gain access to the account to carry out further attacks.

Purpose

The purpose of this playbook is to provide technical insight and guidance into the actions to be considered when investigating and responding to a BEC incident.  

This document is designed for educational settings with some existing cyber security controls. It is not a standalone document and must be used in conjunction with your settings Incident Management Plan (Cyber Response Plan) and associated safeguarding procedures. 

Whilst this document will contain technical actions that can be taken during an incident, it should ideally be reviewed by your technical support team to tailor a response process specifically to your setting. This should be done in advance and kept up to date so that your tailored playbook is ready to be used when an incident occurs.

This document covers a BEC incident affecting a settings IT infrastructure, digital assets and data held within. It is designed to be used by your settings technical IT teams or ICT Managed service provider, alongside existing incident management (cyber response) and safeguarding procedures.  

This document will explain the issue at its highest level, meaning that every aspect may not be relevant to your setting. If you have any system specific issues or queries, these should be addressed to your technical, ICT Managed service provider and/or senior leadership team for additional guidance.

Immediate actions

If you have identified an ongoing attack, first consult your Cyber Response Plan (CRP). This will inform you on your setting’s standard cyber incident response process which this playbook will enhance. This includes the following initial response: 

  • Contact your service provider immediately as they will help with remediation efforts.
  • If you’ve identified a fraudulent payment, contact your bank directly using their official website or phone number.
  • This should also be reported as a crime to Action Fraud by calling 0300 123 2040.
  • If you are a member of the Risk Protection Agreement (RPA) please inform them of the incident.
  • If you have an insurance policy, your insurance company should be contacted. Depending on your insurance agreement, they may assist in remediation plans.

 In order to contain the infection and minimise impact, it is important your technical support or service provider carry out the following immediate actions:

  1. Contact your Digital and Incident SLT to enact your setting’s Cyber Response Plan. If you do not have a BEC specific plan, continue following this process.
    1. If you have been financially impacted, inform your settings bank to prevent further financial damage.
  2. Isolate the affected accounts and/or devices.
       
    1. If you have identified which accounts have been compromised, isolate these accounts by (Please follow Microsoft guidance):
      1. Restricting the ability to send emails (Outlook) to prevent further spread and ensure email forwarding has not be set up
      2. Restrict any additional privileges
      3. Forcefully disabling the user account
        1. This can be done by changing password and login authorisation 
      4. If possible, forcefully sign-out of all active sessions
      5. If your setting has multi-factor authentication (MFA) in place, ensure there are no unauthorised methods added to prevent reinfection 
    2. If a specific device has been impacted (for example, a keylogger virus was installed), then disconnect the device from your settings network as soon as possible. 
      1. This can be by disabling the internet connection of the device or if not possible, disabling the wi-fi to completely isolate the device
      2. Scan the network using an anti-virus to ensure there are no persistent malware on your system
  3. Ensure that the integrity of your settings financial systems (e.g. payroll, HR) have not been severely compromised. This includes:
    1. Administrative accounts
    2. User payroll information
    3. Expected payment amounts
