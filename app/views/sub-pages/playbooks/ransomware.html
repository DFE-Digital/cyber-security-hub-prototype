Disclaimer

If you are currently experiencing a ransomware incident, you should immediately activate your cyber response plan. This will give you specific actions that need to be taken to restore your system.

All settings must now have a 3-2-1 backup process in place which should now be applied. You should contact your IT provider for support or reach out to the RPA if you are a member.

If your incident plan isn’t available or you don’t have a cyber response process, it's crucial to use and adapt the steps in this playbook.

Tailoring the actions to your setting will give you the best possible chance to limit impact and begin recovery to daily operations.

Purpose

The purpose of this playbook is to provide technical insight and guidance into the actions to be considered when detecting, investigating and responding to a ransomware incident.  

This document is designed for educational settings with some existing cyber security controls. It is not a standalone document and must be used in conjunction with your settings Incident Management Plan (Cyber Response Plan) and associated safeguarding procedures. 

Whilst this document will contain technical actions that can be taken during an incident, it should ideally be reviewed by your technical support team to tailor a response process specifically to your setting. 

This document covers a ransomware incident affecting a settings IT infrastructure, digital assets and data held within. It is designed to be used by your organisations technical IT teams or ICT Managed service provider, alongside existing incident management (cyber response) and safeguarding procedures.  

This document will explain the issue at its highest-level, meaning that every aspect may not be relevant to your organisation.

If you have any system specific issues or queries, these should be addressed to your technical, ICT Managed service provider and/or senior leadership team for additional guidance.

Responding to a ransom note

Ransom Message/Payment 

As part of a ransomware incident, you may receive a warning message (also known as a ransom note) informing the user of the infected device that any data on the device has been compromised, encrypted/locked or stolen. This often comes with a monetary value to release your files back to you within a set time frame. 

Likewise, a threat actor may attempt to directly communicate with you and demand a ransom. It is important to slow down and ensure all alternatives to payment are considered without feeling pressured. 

There is no guarantee that your data and device will be unencrypted or disinfected if that ransom is paid. 

Where possible, consult with an expert including RPA (if you are a member), insurers, service providers, the NCSC, law enforcement and/or cyber incident response (CIR) companies with expertise in handling ransomware incidents (please see the NCSC’s list of assured CIR services)

To help with further investigation, take a picture of the message on another device. This may be used as evidence for law enforcement to identify threat actors and potentially the type of ransomware used. 

For additional guidance on whether you should pay, please see the NCSC’s article.

Immediate Actions

If you have identified an ongoing ransomware attack, first follow your Cyber Response Plan (CRP). This will inform you on your settings cyber incident response process. This includes the following initial response: 

  • Contact your service provider immediately as they will help with remediation efforts.
  • If you are a member of the Risk Protection Agreement (RPA) please inform them of the incident. 
  • If you have a cyber insurance policy, your insurance company should be contacted. Depending on your insurance agreement, they may assist in remediation plans. 

To contain the infection and minimise impact, it is important your technical support or service provider carry out the following immediate actions:

  1. Identify all affected systems, starting with the device and system where initial reporting occurred.
    1. This includes understanding critical systems used for daily functionality as to protect them and avoid any further disruption as much as possible.
  2. Notify the SLT digital lead key leadership within your setting and your service provider.
    1. Including your Digital Lead, Incident Lead, Service Provider and, if needed, safeguarding lead.
    2. Utilise your setting’s communication plan if possible.
    3. Urgency is key to preventing a large-scale infection.
    4. Don’t communicate via potentially compromised systems — use your communication plan.
      1. This can include using mobile phones that aren’t connected to internal Wi-Fi or talking face-to-face.
      2. Threat actors will attempt to monitor communications to see if they have been detected, so limiting visibility to communication channels is a priority.
  3. Prioritise isolating critical infrastructure from affected systems
    1. Disconnect affected devices from the network
      1. This can be by physically disconnecting the devices (removing the ethernet cable while following your setting’s safety protocol) or by disabling the Wi-Fi capability (including disconnecting the router).
      2. If this is not possible, power down the device (Note: this will prevent your setting from maintaining key investigation artefacts and evidence stored in volatile memory. Only do this if it is not possible to temporarily shut down the network or disconnect affected devices).
    2. If multiple devices are affected, disable the network to limit the further spread of ransomware
      1. This can be achieved by physically unplugging the Wi-Fi router, only if the infected devices are unable to connect to another network.
      2. If you are unable to disable network or internet connections, you must temporarily disable network shares and shared drives.
        1. Any adjacent file shares should be temporarily disabled to prevent spread.
        2. If this is not possible, contact your service provider for further actions.
      3. If your setting has backup internet connectivity, this should also be disabled on infected devices and systems to prevent further infection.
    3. As mentioned in a.ii, do not power off the machine unless it is specified in your cyber response process or you have been told to by your SLT incident lead, so that you retain any evidence.
  4. Block unknown malicious IP addresses, domains and website URLs at your networks firewall.
    1. This is important on all external-facing networks (please refer to your provider for system-specific information. At a minimum, Windows systems have the following capabilities)
    2. This can be done by enabling an allow-list (aka whitelist) of known internal IP addresses

Reporting

All cyber incidents should be reported immediately to the relevant authority. As a ransomware incident has likely resulted in some form of data breach, the following external supervisory authorities should be informed: 

Information Commissioners Office

This must be done within 72 hours of becoming aware of your incident/data breach. 

This includes the nature of the breach, categories and number of individuals data that has been affected.

National cyber security centre (NCSC)

If there is a clear danger to safeguarding for either staff members or leaners, please contact NCSC and fill out the form.

Local Police Authorities

Please reach out to your local police authority if there is suspected clear financial or safeguarding impact.

Action Fraud

You can visit their website or call 0300 123 2040

DfE RPA Team

If you are a member of the RPA membership scheme, please contact the RPA Emergency Assistance Helpline by emailing RPAresponse@CyberClan.com or call 0800 368 6378.

Service Providers

If the breach has affected any third-party service/system, the service providers should be informed of the attack and involved in a coordinated response to the incident. 

Insurance Providers 

If you are a member of a private insurance and cyber incidents are part of your coverage, please contact them immediately. Dependant on your coverage, they may provide direct assistance in recovery. 

In the event your incident has been discovered and reported by a news media outlet, it is important that you have assigned a staff member to co-ordinate communications in line with an approved post-disaster communication plan. The staff media contact should only provide verifiable information, with no room for speculation.  

For information that is still not known, it is justified to simply state “That is not known at this stage”. Only the appointed staff media contact should communicate with the media, with all other staff refusing to comment.

Containment

Further Containment

In some serious cases, your Incident Lead should consider whether turning off key internet and any core network connections (including network hardware such as routers and switches) may be necessary. 

It is important to identify and discuss the following actions with your SLT incident lead, technical support and service providers when containing an incident to minimise impact: 

  • Identify all impacted devices and systems impacted by the malware, and where the initial incident occurred
  • Prioritise isolating critical systems that are essential to daily operations
  • This includes systems that hold learner data, financial systems and any systems that may affect safeguarding
  • Check if your setting has an asset list to help with this 
  • If taking the network down temporarily offline is not possible, remove any infected (even if suspected) devices offline by removing internet/network connectivity.
  • For cloud environments, take a digital image snapshot using the relevant tooling of affected volumes to get a point-in-time copy for further forensic investigation.

After a compromise, threat actors may still be on the system and monitor your settings activity/communications to understand if they have been detected. Isolate any suspected impacted systems in a coordinated manner and use alternate communication (over the phone or in-person) to communicate.

Eradication and Recovery

Prior to taking any recovery action, ensure any details of the incident are recorded and documented in detail for further evidence. 

Once all devices and systems infected by the ransomware have been identified and further infection has been mitigated, they can undergo decontamination and recovery prior to reconnecting to your network.

Please be aware, critical files that have been encrypted by most ransomware have no way of being decrypted by anyone other than the threat actor or by expert intervention (see Trend Micro Decryption Tool for potential decryption methods).  

If critical systems or data have been encrypted, it may be tempting to pay the ransom. There is no guarantee that your devices, systems or data will be decrypted.   There are online resources that provide collections of decryption tools, this includes the No More Ransom Project and other anti-virus vendors (e.g TrendMicro).  

If critical infrastructure has not been infected, the following recovery actions should be taken: 

  1. Reset any account credentials/passwords, especially for administrator or system accounts, that may have been impacted.
    1. This can include resetting all accounts to be safe.
  2. Safely restart and wipe the infected devices to reinstall the operating system.
  3. Prior to restoring from a backup or connecting the device to your network, verify it is free of any malware. 
    1. This includes using software anti-virus and triaging all devices
    2. Only connect devices to your network and restore from a backup if you are very confident that the backup and device are not infected
    3. Consider utilising a phased backup approach. This includes backing up devices and systems in waves, rather than all at once.
      1. This can limit the spread of an infection if any remains whilst restoration is ongoing.
  4. Connect devices to a clean network to download, install and update the operating system and any additional software.
  5. Once all security operating system and software updates are installed, run local anti-virus scans on all devices.
    1. Once this has returned a clean scan, the device can be reconnected to your network.
  6. Continually monitor network traffic and run anti-virus scans frequently to identify any remaining malicious presence.

For additional information on removing virus infections, please follow NCSC Guidance.  

Depending on the nature of the incident, threat actors may have hidden additional access to infected systems (known as a “backdoor”). Ensure all aspects of the system have been inspected and triaged including backup volumes, administrator storage and unlikely sources of network activity.  

This can be verified by performing system anti-malware scans and thorough system checks (either by internal or external auditing teams). This must include:

  • A thorough vulnerability scan on all devices attached to your settings network. 
  • Checking that all software and hardware is licensed and received up-to-date functionality and security updates/patches. 

For more technical guidance on triaging and remediating malicious activity, please see a publication released by CISA and NCSC.

Post-Incident

Debrief

It is important that all members of staff involved in incident investigation, triaging, mitigation and recovery undergo some form of interview and action review to ensure all the appropriate steps were taken to mitigate the incident.  

This includes communicating to all stakeholders internal to your setting (e.g. Digital Leader, Senior Leadership, safeguarding officer), and external to your investigation if they have been impacted (e.g. parent/learner data will require legal guardians to be informed “without undue delay”, according to GDPR). 

Ensure your incident review report is readable and concise to help present to senior leadership. It is key to have full support in the actions taken during and post-incident as to prevent a ‘blame culture’ and ensure that the right steps were taken. 

It is important to remember that whilst the initial incident has been mitigated and system recovered, all users and incident leader should remain vigilant and aware of possible future compromise. This should be involved in any debriefs, training activities and lead to a review/update of your setting cyber response plan and cyber risk assessment.
