A cyber response plan lets you plan now for a future situation. Your plan should meet your needs and reflect your educational setting, cyber experience and security resources.

You should also review and test your plan. The DfE cyber response guidance has suggestions for how to do this.

You should include these elements in your response plan.

Purpose

Briefly explain the purpose of your plan. Your plan should help you manage and respond to cyber incidents to:

  • minimise harm
  • restore services
  • comply with legal and regulatory duties

Other plans, policies and procedures

Make sure you know of other plans, policies or procedures that might be relevant to your plan. These might include:

  • disaster recovery plan for technical recovery
  • business continuity plan for maintaining education delivery
  • safeguarding policy for incidents involving students
  • data protection and privacy policy for incidents involving potential loss of personal information

If these plans exist for your educational setting, make sure you refer to them and include them in your plan. You may want to think about how they work together and if there are other people who need to be involved.

Objectives

List the objectives of the plan. They should include:

  • detect and respond to cyber security incidents quickly and effectively
  • contain and eradicate threats
  • recover affected systems and data
  • meet legal obligations, for example data breach notification to the ICO
  • capture lessons learned

You may want to add other objectives related to your educational setting or set by your leadership team.

Scope

Set out what is and is not in the scope of your plan.

Preparing to handle incidents

Your plan should set out who will be involved in a cyber response. A roles and responsibilities section can make clear:

  • who should be involved and when
  • roles and responsibilities for people involved

The DfE cyber response guidance lists roles that need to be carried out in an incident.

Make sure the people named in your plan can make decisions and take action. They will need to do things like:

  • decide when to escalate incidents to senior stakeholders
  • take action and run an incident response
  • identify when IT can contain an incident without having to contact other stakeholders

List who will be involved in your response and their responsibilities.

Communication

You should have a communication plan and pre prepared communications you can use in an incident. DfE cyber response guidelines have guidance and templates for communications.

Prepare these now, have them approved by stakeholders and include them in your cyber response plan. Make sure they are saved somewhere you can access them if your IT fails.

Record keeping

DfE cyber response guidance has guidance on how to keep records during an incident.

Include templates in your plan and make sure they fit your organisation’s needs and capabilities.

Incident response process

DfE cyber response guidance has guidance on the process for responding to an incident. It also has a checklist.

Your plan should explain what happens in each step. You can customise the steps so they suit your educational setting.

The incident response process is:

  1. Detection and reporting
    • staff report incidents via email or helpdesk
    • IT team detect or are alerted to potential incidents
  2. Triage and analysis
    • assess severity and identify incident type
    • initiate playbook if relevant
  3. Containment
    • isolate affected systems and accounts
  4. Eradication
    • remove the threat, for example malware removal
  5. Recovery
    • restore from backup
    • verify integrity
  6. Notification
    • notify affected users
    • notify the ICO if required
  7. Post incident review
    • document what happened
    • capture lessons learned

Detection and reporting

Explain the ways cyber incidents will be detected and reported in your educational setting.

The DfE cyber response guidance has examples.

Triage and classification

Explain how you will establish:

  • what kind of cyber incident is happening
  • how serious the incident is

The DfE cyber response guidance has guidance and examples.

You can also find DfE playbooks with incident response processes. You may want to include these, and how you will decide to use them, in your response.

Containment

This section sets out how you will:

  • limit the spread of the incident
  • preserve evidence
  • minimise disruption
  • protect sensitive data

The DfE cyber response guidance has a general containment process that you can tailor to your needs and requirements.

Eradication

The eradication phase is where you remove the cause of the incident and any residual components and make sure it cannot happen again.

The DfE cyber response guidance has an example process you can tailor to your requirements.

Recovery

The recovery section will explain how you will restore your systems, data and services to normal.

The DfE cyber response guidance has an example process you can tailor to your education setting.

Notification

Your notification section will explain how you inform your stakeholders about the incident.

The DfE cyber response guidance has an example notification process for incidents. You can adapt this to your own education setting.

Post incident review

Explain what you will do after an incident to:

  • evaluate your response
  • identify lessons learned
  • make improvements to reduce the chance of further attacks

The DfE cyber response guidance has an example review process you can adapt to your education setting.

Approval

Make sure your plan includes:

  • who approved it
  • the date it was approved
  • when it should be reviewed next
